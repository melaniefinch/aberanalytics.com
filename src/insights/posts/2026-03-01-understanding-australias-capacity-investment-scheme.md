The Capacity Investment Scheme (CIS) represents a fundamental shift in how Australia underwrites new generation and storage investment. Designed to provide revenue certainty for projects that the market alone may not deliver on time, the CIS operates as a federal mechanism layered on top of the NEM's existing energy-only market structure.

## How the CIS works

At its core, the CIS is a contracts-for-difference (CfD) mechanism. Successful projects receive a guaranteed floor price for their output — if market revenues fall below the strike price, the Commonwealth makes up the difference. If market revenues exceed the strike price, the project pays back the surplus.

This two-way structure is critical. Unlike a one-way floor (which would simply be a subsidy), the CfD aligns incentives: projects still benefit from generating during high-price periods, but the taxpayer is protected from overpaying during sustained bull markets.

### Key design features

- **Competitive auctions** determine the strike price, ensuring price discovery rather than administered pricing
- **Technology-neutral tenders** for dispatchable capacity (storage, gas peakers) and variable renewables (wind, solar)
- **Revenue adequacy periods** of 10-15 years, long enough to underwrite project finance but short enough to avoid locking in stale assumptions
- **Regional allocation** guided by AEMO's Integrated System Plan, targeting the zones with greatest need

## What it means for investment decisions

The CIS changes the risk calculus for project developers and investors in several important ways.

### Merchant exposure reduction

Without the CIS, a new wind or solar project faces full merchant price risk from commissioning. Our modelling shows that merchant exposure varies dramatically across scenarios — a project that looks viable under AEMO's Step Change pathway may be deeply underwater in a slower transition scenario.

The CIS truncates the left tail of the revenue distribution. For projects that secure a CfD, the downside is bounded: even in a world of sustained low wholesale prices (driven by renewable oversupply), the floor price provides a minimum revenue stream.

### Implications for non-CIS projects

Here's where it gets interesting. Projects that *don't* receive a CIS contract now face a more competitive market. The CIS-backed projects can bid at lower prices (their revenue floor reduces risk premiums), which means non-CIS projects face potentially lower capture prices.

This creates a bifurcation in the market:

- **CIS projects**: Lower risk, lower return, bankable revenue streams
- **Merchant projects**: Higher risk, potentially higher return, but dependent on favourable market conditions

### Portfolio considerations

For large portfolios — including entities like the CEFC — the CIS introduces interesting optimisation questions:

- What is the optimal mix of CIS-backed and merchant assets?
- How does the CIS interact with existing PPA structures?
- Should storage assets target CIS contracts, or is the optionality of the merchant market more valuable?

These are exactly the kinds of questions that probabilistic modelling is designed to answer. A single deterministic scenario cannot capture the value of the CIS floor — you need to model the full distribution of outcomes and see how the floor changes the shape of that distribution.

## The broader market impact

The CIS is expected to accelerate investment in the NEM, but the second-order effects are nuanced:

1. **Firming investment** — CIS tenders for dispatchable capacity directly address the "missing money" problem for storage and flexible generation
2. **Transmission dependency** — CIS projects in REZs need transmission to be built on time; delays create basis risk even with a CfD
3. **Price suppression** — More subsidised renewables entering the market could suppress wholesale prices further, increasing the importance of CIS contracts for subsequent projects

## Our view

The CIS is well-designed for its stated purpose: accelerating investment to meet Australia's 82% renewable target. But the full implications depend on how it interacts with state-level schemes (like Victoria's VRET), transmission build timelines, and the pace of coal retirement.
